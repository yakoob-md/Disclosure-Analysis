PART 4 — SEVEN GENUINE RESEARCH GAPS
These are not "gaps" in the generic sense. These are specific, verifiable absences in the literature that your project can credibly address.
Gap 1 — Temporal Drift in Disclosure Behavior is Unmeasured
No study has formally modeled how individual employees' disclosure patterns change as a function of organizational stress. Specifically: does disclosure of sensitive financial information increase or decrease as the company approaches bankruptcy? Does it become more targeted (fewer recipients) or more diffuse? This is a core criminology question — and it has never been studied computationally on Enron with proper temporal resolution.
Feasibility: High. Doable with month-by-month slicing and centrality trajectory plots.
Gap 2 — Role-Appropriateness Scoring for Disclosure
No existing model checks whether the content of an email matches the authorized role of its sender in the organizational hierarchy. A "trader" discussing "legal strategy" with an "external party" should be flagged differently than a "lawyer" doing the same. This requires a KG that encodes role permissions — something no existing Enron paper does.
Feasibility: Medium-High. Requires constructing a role-permission hierarchy from the organizational chart.
Gap 3 — Multi-Label Disclosure Classification
As established above, all existing classification work is binary. Multi-label classification of disclosure type (financial, PII, strategic, relational) across a unified model has not been done.
Feasibility: High. Standard multi-label classification with BCE loss.
Gap 4 — Graph Neural Networks for Disclosure Detection on Enron
GNNs (specifically Graph Convolutional Networks and Graph Attention Networks) have been applied to social network anomaly detection, but not to Enron for the specific task of disclosure analysis. The KG you construct is a perfect substrate for a GCN that treats each email as a node with features from both text embeddings and graph topology.
Feasibility: Medium. Requires PyTorch Geometric or DGL.
Gap 5 — LLM Consistency Under Prompt Variation for Forensic Labeling
Existing work uses LLMs for zero-shot classification but does not study prompt sensitivity — i.e., how much does the LLM's disclosure classification change when the prompt is reworded? In a forensic context, a system that changes its verdict based on prompt wording is not legally defensible. This stability analysis has never been performed on Enron.
Feasibility: High. Test 5-10 prompt variants on 100 emails, measure agreement.
Gap 6 — Centrality as a Dynamic Feature (Not Static)
Every paper that uses centrality metrics computes them once on the entire graph. Nobody has studied what happens when you compute centrality on rolling 30-day windows and use the trajectory (rate of change) of centrality as a feature. A sudden spike in betweenness centrality may be more predictive of risk than a sustained high betweenness.
Feasibility: High. Requires temporal windowing in NetworkX, straightforward implementation.
Gap 7 — Cross-Silo Disclosure Path Detection via Multi-Hop KG Reasoning
The most sophisticated gap. No paper has defined and detected "unauthorized information pathways" in the Enron KG — i.e., paths where sensitive information travels from a high-authority node, through a broker node, to an external entity, via multiple intermediary hops. GraphRAG approaches exist but have not been applied to this specific forensic framing.
Feasibility: Medium. Requires careful Cypher query design in Neo4j and path scoring.

PART 7 — METHODOLOGY: THE REASONING BEHIND EVERY STEP
A paper reviewer reads your methodology section and asks at each step: "Why did you make this choice and not an alternative?" You must have an answer.
Why temporal split instead of random split?
Because the goal is detection of future risk. A model trained on post-crisis data that "predicts" pre-crisis events is a time-traveler, not a detector. The temporal split reflects the actual deployment scenario and makes your results meaningful and generalizable.
Why multi-label instead of binary?
Because real disclosure risk is not binary. A financial disclosure to an internal colleague is low risk. The same disclosure to an external party is high risk. A binary label collapses this distinction and produces a model that cannot distinguish severity. Multi-label classification also produces a richer confusion matrix that reveals model failure modes.
Why centrality derivatives instead of raw centrality?
Because an employee who has always had high betweenness (a natural hub) is not anomalous. An employee whose betweenness suddenly spikes by 300% in a single month is. The rate of change carries the anomaly signal; the absolute value carries only the structural position signal. Both are valuable, but only one captures behavioral change.
Why construct the KG before training models?
Because the KG is not just a visualization tool — it is a structured source of features and constraints. The role-hierarchy in the KG defines what constitutes "unauthorized" disclosure. Without it, your classifier has no way to evaluate context-appropriateness; it can only detect suspicious language. Language is necessary but not sufficient for disclosure detection.
Why include LLMs in zero-shot, few-shot, and chain-of-thought variants?
Because comparing "LLM" as a single condition is not rigorous. Chain-of-thought prompting often produces qualitatively different reasoning than zero-shot. Documenting all three conditions tells you: (a) what the model can do without examples, (b) how much labeled data as few-shot examples helps, and (c) whether explicit reasoning instructions improve structured tasks. This is research-grade LLM evaluation, not just "I ran GPT-4."
